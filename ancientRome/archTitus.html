Arch of Titus

Historical Background

The Arch of Titus is an ancient Roman triumphal arch built in Rome around 81 CE. It stands near the Roman Forum and is one of the oldest surviving arches of its kind. It reflects the power of the Roman Empire during the early imperial period.

  • Built in ancient Rome
  • Dates to around 81 CE
  • Located near the Roman Forum
  • One of the earliest Roman triumphal arches

Reason for Construction

The arch was built to commemorate Emperor Titus’s victory in the siege of Jerusalem in 70 CE. It celebrates Rome’s military success and was meant to remind viewers of Roman strength and authority.

  • Honors Emperor Titus
  • Celebrates victory in Jerusalem
  • Marks the end of the Jewish revolt
  • Used as Roman propaganda

Arch Builders

The arch was commissioned by Emperor Domitian, Titus’s brother, after Titus’s death. It was designed and built by Roman architects and craftsmen using high-quality materials.

  • Commissioned by Emperor Domitian
  • Built by Roman architects and workers
  • Made mainly from marble

Design of Arch

The Arch of Titus has a simple but powerful Roman design that emphasizes strength and victory. It is a single-arched monument decorated with classical elements such as columns and relief sculptures. The balanced proportions and rich carvings reflect the architectural style of the early Roman Empire.

  • Single arch (not multiple passageways)
  • Features Corinthian columns on each side
  • Decorated with relief sculptures inside the archway
  • Designed to appear monumental and symmetrical

Relief on Arch

The relief sculptures on the Arch of Titus show scenes celebrating Rome’s victory over Jerusalem. One relief depicts Roman soldiers carrying sacred treasures from the Jewish Temple, including the Menorah, during a triumphal procession. Another relief shows Emperor Titus riding in a chariot, symbolizing his military success and divine approval. These carvings were meant to glorify the emperor and demonstrate the power of Rome.
